On Colors

by Zucchini Li


What's your favorite color?

I can recall being asked that a surprising number of times just in the past year never mind the whole 19 years I've been alive. And each time I never have a response. What is my favorite color? Honestly I'm not sure. My closet consists of mostly monotone combinations of black and whites, my rose gold iPhone is covered in a black case and even the lock screen has been filtered so that the bright red wall in the backdrop is now a dark grey. It's not that I don't like color, it's just that I never really think about it either. It's convenient not having to worry about walking around all day in clashing colors. I simply get dressed faster in the morning, the black case was the one most readily available to me, and the grey filter gives the photo a nice aesthetic.

Perhaps its this lack of color that I recently noticed that has piqued my interest in color. I have a mustard colored coat that had been gathering dust in the closet for almost a year after I deemed it too bright despite only a few wears. I bought the coat because it's a beautiful color but somehow I had forgotten to appreciate that fact. I finally pulled it out again this fall and it gives me as much joy to put on now as my black jeans. Though I won't go as far as to say its like I'm seeing color for the first time, I have been considering them, dwelling on their taste, if you will.

Color has a strong effect on us. Just look at the colorless dystopian utopias of Lois Lowry's The Giver and the film Equals, featuring the white clad Kristen Stewart and Nicholas Hoult living and working in buildings of some nondescript shade of white or gray. Somehow the two so called utopians lack the very thing that makes life vibrant. Sure there are some beautiful scenes of the forest in Equals and Hoult's character draws up some amazing illustrations, but the government has done their best to remove what color and emotions they can from their citizens lives. When young Jonas first sees color in The Giver he is more perplexed than anything else. He doesn't even have a word for what he sees. He can only say at first that "the apple had changed." The world he lives in has no capacity for color or pain. The citizens choose instead to push all the memories of the past onto one man called The Giver. But with the pain goes the colors and joy, because without one, there are no others. Or consider the dull colored dystopians who don't pretend to be anything other than what they are, like Cormac McCarthy's relentlessly gray world of The Road. The only image of hope in this world is fire, red, one of the very few colors that do appear reapetedly, though sometimes in the form of blood. What other little color there is shows up mainly in memories of the past or in dreams, both places not of the desolate present. So we see that the repression of color makes for a bleak picture.

Yet when we have color in abundance we seem to take it for granted, rarely consciously considering it. Everlane, an online retailer that has quickly risen in popularity, currently has only a very minimal number of colorful pieces in their collection. And recently when I started asking people what they thought of color, the response was always confusion. "What do you mean?" Once, after clarifying, I even got the response "distractive." I don't want to say that everyone takes color for granted -- there are, after all, people whose livelihood depends on their connection with color -- but generally speaking, we rarely do think of color.

But maybe the confusion I've received in response to my question isn't merely because we tend not to think about color, but rather because we wouldn't know how, even if we wanted to, to think about color in such a way that it is something in itself, not merely an attribute of something else, like the blueness of a chair. Because how do you describe color to someone who cannot see? If you said, color is a property, a wavelength, what would that even mean? It does not even come close to the beauty of color and the things that color can do. If you had to describe color in nonscientific and non definitional terms in such a way that color is something in itself, then how would you do it? I am reminded of a poem, The Problem of Describing Color by Robert Hass. Though his description is not necesarily meant for the blind, Hass has done better than most of us probably can: "flecks of poppy in the tar-grass scented summer air/ On a wind-struck hillside outside Fano â€“ " What an image. Almost as if red is the poppy and not the other way around. But Hass' answer is not perfect either as the title of his work would suggests.

Now I'm not saying that I have the answer and that I know how to think about color as something in itself, because I don't. I'm merely suggesting that there is more than one way to think about and see color and right now most of us are just barely scratching the surface at what there is to see. As the artist in Alva Noe's anecdote says, "[W]e see so little, when there is so much around us to see," (Strange Tools).

My goal in this project is to get people to see a little more, to force an awareness of color on them, to see how words can illustrate images that evoke emotions that remind us of colors which then turns around and feeds those emotions.
